Quick Synthesis of the Design

▼

1.1 Overall Vision

Plan International Kenya envisions COSME Phase 2 as a transformative evolution from a strong implementation consortium into a cohesive, learning-oriented, and influence-driven partnership that delivers measurable improvements in women's and youth's lives, coastal ecosystem sustainability, and governance systems. The collaboration logic centres on a hybrid model combining geographic coordination with technical specialization: Plan International Kenya leads in established geographies with strengths in gender justice, regenerative agriculture, enterprise development, and conservation governance, while MEDA, AECF, Natural State, and CICaN contribute complementary technical expertise in processing, value addition, market access, conservation, and institutional coordination. All five partners share strategic functions — research, MEL, policy advocacy, safeguarding, and institutional strengthening — to ensure coherence, reduce duplication, and amplify collective impact through sequenced and layered interventions across the value chain.

1.2 Key Problems / Gaps from Phase 1

  • Fragmented value chains and low production volumes — interventions were insufficiently integrated across production, value addition, and markets.
  • Uneven institutional capacity among women- and youth-led groups, limiting sustainability and scale readiness.
  • Gaps between community-level practice and policy frameworks — limited translation of community innovation into governance and budget influence.
  • Livelihood gains alone proved insufficient without parallel governance and institutional change.
  • Gender participation without transformation — women participated but agency, leadership, and norm change were not systematically embedded.
  • Disconnected interventions — activities lacked the sequenced, layered logic needed for durable impact.
  • Institutional fragmentation and limited local capacity among coordination platforms and government forums.
  • Safeguarding and GESI mainstreaming not yet systematically embedded across all partner activities.

1.2b Key Opportunities for Phase 2

  • Strong potential for women-led enterprises when supported holistically (demonstrated in Phase 1).
  • Devolution creates county-level entry points for policy engagement, budgeting, and blue economy governance.
  • Growing blue economy discourse nationally and regionally increases political will and donor interest.
  • Increased climate finance interest offers new resource mobilization, co-financing, and carbon/biodiversity readiness opportunities through measurement and verification systems.
  • Established trust and community systems from Phase 1 provide a foundation for deepening.
  • COSME Phase 1 evidence base can be leveraged for advocacy, adaptive programming, and influence.
  • Partner complementarity — clear, non-duplicative roles across the five consortium members.
  • Gender Justice Journey model can be upgraded from household level to group and enterprise level.

2.2 Background and Rationale

Context of Coastal Communities and Blue Economy Opportunities

Kenya's coastal counties — including Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, and Tana River — are home to communities whose livelihoods depend on marine and coastal resources, including seaweed farming, mangrove-linked enterprises, beekeeping, tourism, crab farming, and small-scale fisheries. Despite the significant economic potential of the blue economy, coastal communities face persistent poverty, gender inequality, limited market access, and escalating climate risks. Women and youth, who form the backbone of coastal production systems, remain disproportionately excluded from decision-making, value chain governance, and equitable economic returns. Kenya's devolution framework, growing national blue economy discourse, and emerging climate finance opportunities (carbon/biodiversity measurement and verification) present an unprecedented opportunity to address these structural barriers at county and national levels.

Phase 1 Achievements and Key Learnings

COSME Phase 1 established a solid implementation foundation, demonstrating that women-led enterprises thrive when supported holistically with production inputs, savings (VSLAs), skills development, and mentorship. Phase 1 built community trust, established women- and youth-led groups, piloted the Gender Justice Journey at household level, initiated seaweed and mangrove-linked livelihoods, and generated an evidence base on coastal livelihoods and gender dynamics. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical limitations: fragmented value chains, low production volumes, uneven institutional capacity among community groups, and insufficient linkage between community innovation and policy influence.

Gaps and Challenges Justifying Phase 2

Despite Phase 1 progress, fundamental gaps persist. Value chains remain fragmented, with weak aggregation, quality standards, and market linkages. Community institutions — WROs, YLOs, cooperatives — have variable governance, financial management, and sustainability capacity. Gender participation has not yet translated into systematic norm change, agency, or leadership at enterprise and governance levels. Policy engagement remains ad hoc, with insufficient use of evidence to influence county and national budgets and plans. Safeguarding systems require strengthening across the consortium. These gaps underscore the need for a dedicated second phase.

Why a Gender-Transformative, Systems-Strengthening, and Market Systems Approach Is Needed

Addressing poverty and inequality in coastal Kenya requires more than livelihood support; it demands transformation of the systems — social, economic, institutional, and political — that perpetuate exclusion. A gender-transformative approach moves beyond women's access to resources to tackle harmful norms, power relations, unpaid care work, and decision-making barriers. A systems-strengthening approach builds the capacity, accountability, and sustainability of community institutions, government forums, and coordination platforms. A market systems approach facilitates private sector linkages, enabling women and youth to participate in markets on equitable terms.

2.3 Overall Goal and Specific Objectives

Overall Goal (Impact Level)

To improve the economic well-being, resilience, and agency of women and youth in coastal Kenya through inclusive, gender-transformative, and climate-resilient blue economy livelihoods, strengthened market systems, and accountable governance.

Pillar 1

Climate-Resilient Coastal Livelihoods

To increase the productivity, diversification, and climate resilience of coastal livelihood systems — particularly seaweed production (technology, seed supply, basic value addition, deep sea expansion), mangrove restoration and conservation (hectare expansion, community-led, green assets), upstream forest conservation, and other nature-based enterprises (beekeeping, tourism, crab farming) — for women and youth in target communities. This pillar includes climate finance readiness through carbon/biodiversity measurement and verification systems.

Pillar 2

Women's Economic Empowerment & Gender Justice

To strengthen the economic agency, leadership, decision-making power, and collective action of women and girls through gender-transformative programming, including VSLAs, enterprise governance, upskilling and capacity building, and the upgraded Gender Justice Journey. This pillar centers on gender transformation — moving beyond participation to address norms, power, and unpaid care.

Pillar 3

Market Systems & Value Chain Strengthening

To facilitate improved value addition, quality standards, aggregation, and inclusive market linkages for coastal products. Scaling is pursued through organizational structures (cooperatives, clusters, associations) with strategic value chain upgrading — including high-value market access, pull-effect/lead firm approaches, and access to alternative capital and financing.

Pillar 4

Governance, Policy Influence & Institutional Capacity

To strengthen community institutions (WROs, YLOs, cooperatives, BMUs), coordination platforms, and evidence-based policy engagement on blue economy, climate adaptation, and gender-responsive governance. This includes monitoring, evaluation and research; local community-based advocacy through community-based organizations; and direct advocacy — lobbying parliamentarians at regional and national levels on strategic policy needs related to conservation and economic empowerment.

2.4 Theory of Change

a) Narrative Theory of Change

COSME Phase 2 is premised on the understanding that sustainable, inclusive economic empowerment for coastal women and youth requires simultaneous and sequenced investment across four interconnected domains: climate-resilient production, gender-transformative empowerment, inclusive market systems, and accountable governance.

If women and youth receive sustained support in climate-smart production, business skills, and financial services (VSLAs), and if gender-transformative programming addresses norms, power relations, and unpaid care at group and enterprise levels, then women and youth will increase their productivity and assume leadership roles. If value chain interventions strengthen aggregation, quality, and market access, then women- and youth-led enterprises will capture greater value. If community institutions are strengthened and evidence is used for coordinated advocacy, then policy frameworks will become more inclusive and responsive. Gender justice, safeguarding, and systems-strengthening are embedded throughout the causal chain.

b) Theory of Change Flow

⬇ IF (Inputs & Activities)

  • Plan International Kenya delivers gender-transformative programming (GJJ upgrade, leadership, VSLA strengthening, enterprise governance, upskilling).
  • Plan co-leads community institutional strengthening (WROs, YLOs, cooperatives, BMUs) and coordination platforms.
  • MEDA & AECF facilitate market access, strategic value chain upgrading (value addition, high-value market access, lead firm approach), and private sector partnerships.
  • Natural State & CICaN support production systems (seaweed, mangrove restoration, upstream forest conservation), technical training, and institutional coordination.
  • All partners jointly deliver research, MEL, advocacy, safeguarding, and learning.
  • Climate-smart production support for seaweed, mangrove-linked, and nature-based enterprises (beekeeping, tourism, crab farming).
  • Climate finance readiness — carbon/biodiversity measurement and verification systems established.
  • Scaling through organizations (cooperatives, clusters, associations) with access to alternative capital and financing.
⬇

⬇ THEN (Outputs)

  • Women & youth trained in climate-resilient production, business skills, and financial literacy.
  • Gender Justice Journey delivered at group and enterprise levels.
  • VSLAs strengthened and linked to enterprise development and financial services.
  • Value addition, quality standards, and aggregation systems established.
  • Community institutions strengthened; safeguarding systems operational.
  • Joint advocacy agendas developed; policy engagement initiated.
⬇

⬇ WHICH LEADS TO (Intermediate Outcomes)

  • Increased and more resilient incomes for women and youth.
  • Improved agency, leadership, and decision-making power of women.
  • More sustainable management of coastal ecosystems.
  • Women- and youth-led enterprises integrated into inclusive markets.
  • Community institutions accountable and linked to county structures.
  • Evidence-informed, responsive governance frameworks.
⬇

⬇ CONTRIBUTING TO (Long-Term Impact)

  • Improved economic well-being, resilience, and gender justice for coastal women, youth, and their communities.
  • Inclusive, sustainable blue economy governance at county and national levels.
  • Systems-level change sustained through strengthened institutions.

2.5 Target Groups and Coverage

Primary Target Groups

  • Women in coastal communities: Members of women-led producer groups, cooperatives, VSLAs, WROs, and BMUs engaged in seaweed, mangrove-linked, and conservation enterprises.
  • Youth (focus on young women and adolescent girls): Members of YLOs, youth enterprise groups, and blue economy pathway participants.
  • Women-led and youth-led enterprises: Groups and cooperatives producing, processing, and trading coastal products.

Secondary / Indirect Target Groups

  • Households of primary participants.
  • Community leaders, men, and boys engaged through GJJ.
  • Private sector actors in value addition and market access.
  • County and national government institutions.
  • Community-based conservation groups and BMUs.

Geographic Focus

  • Core counties: Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, Tana River.
  • Strategy: Deepen in Phase 1 geographies; selective expansion based on ecological suitability and governance readiness.
  • Clustering: Groups clustered for peer learning, aggregation, and market viability.

Collaboration & Systems-Strengthening Architecture

▼

5.1 Consortium Collaboration Model

COSME Phase 2 adopts a hybrid collaboration model that combines geographic coordination with technical specialization and shared strategic functions. Each partner leads in specific geographies where they have established trust and operational systems, while contributing distinct technical strengths that are sequenced and layered across the value chain. Interventions are intentionally connected: communities move from capacity building → production → value addition → market integration → policy influence, with different partners contributing at different stages.

Critical cross-cutting components — including research, MEL, learning, policy advocacy, safeguarding, and institutional strengthening — are jointly designed and delivered by all five partners to ensure coherence and amplify collective impact. Plan International Kenya plays a soft leadership role in consortium coordination, promoting alignment and trust-building, while acting as a connector between technical partners, community actors, and government institutions.

Boundaries, Limitations & Assumptions

▼

6.1 Scope Boundaries

  • Thematic focus: Coastal and marine-based livelihood systems (seaweed, mangrove-linked, conservation enterprises) — not broad livelihood diversification.
  • Target group: Existing women- and youth-led groups, clustered for aggregation and peer learning — not general population.
  • Market engagement: Facilitation of market access through MEDA, AECF, and private sector — Plan does not operate as a commercial entity.
  • Geographic scope: Deepen in Phase 1 geographies; selective expansion only where conditions support it.

6.2 Realistic Change Ambition

✅ What the project WILL achieve

  • Inclusive market participation for women- and youth-led enterprises.
  • Measurable improvements in women's agency, leadership, and decision-making.
  • Stronger, accountable community institutions linked to county structures.
  • Evidence-based policy influence at county level, with national entry points.
  • Operational safeguarding, GESI, and learning systems across the consortium.

What remains BEYOND scope

  • Full transformation of national export markets.
  • Systemic resolution of all climate risks.
  • Complete national-level policy reform on blue economy and gender.
  • Reaching all coastal communities across Kenya's entire coastline.
  • Replacing government or private sector roles.

6.3 Key Assumptions

  • Government support: Continued political will in blue economy and devolution processes.
  • Climate manageability: Climate shocks remain within adaptable levels.
  • Political stability: Minimum stability enables program operations and policy dialogue.
  • Partner continuity: All five consortium partners maintain capacity and commitment.
  • Community willingness: Target communities remain engaged in gender-transformative programming.
  • Market viability: Sufficient demand for coastal products.
  • Civic space: CSOs, WROs, YLOs can continue to organize and advocate.
  • Donor alignment: Donor expectations align with systems-strengthening and adaptive management approach.

MEL, Research, Learning & Adaptive Management

▼

7.1 MEL Approach

  • Harmonized indicators and joint learning across all five COSME partners, with shared MEL framework, common data collection tools, and unified reporting.
  • Outcome harvesting to capture emergent and systemic outcomes (gender norms, institutional capacity, policy influence).
  • Participatory monitoring engaging women and youth groups in data collection, reflection, and feedback.
  • Feedback loops from communities to program design, including complaint and feedback mechanisms.
  • GESI markers and indicators across all activities (economic + gender-transformative dimensions).
  • Safeguarding monitoring integrated and aligned with Plan International's commitments.
  • Data disaggregation by sex, age, county, value chain, and group type.
  • Quarterly and annual reflection events for data review, adaptation, and learning.

7.2 Research & Learning Agenda

Research Priorities

  • Participatory action research on gender, climate resilience, and coastal livelihoods — conducted with and for communities.
  • What works for women-led enterprises — success factors, barriers, and scalable models.
  • Social norms research — how norms, power relations, and unpaid care interact with economic empowerment.
  • Climate resilience — adaptive strategies, risk reduction innovations, ecosystem co-benefits.

Learning Products

  • Case studies, stories of change, good practice briefs, policy-relevant research reports.
  • Community-generated evidence (photo-voice, scorecards, most significant change stories).

Learning Loops & Adaptation

  • Findings fed back through quarterly reflection meetings and annual learning reviews.
  • Learning agenda updated annually with priorities from both planned research and outcome harvesting.
  • Community and partner capacity strengthened to use data for decision-making.

Executive Summary

▼

COSME Phase 2: Advancing Climate-Resilient Blue Economy Livelihoods, Gender Justice, and Systems Change for Coastal Women and Youth in Kenya

Background and Problem. Coastal communities in Kenya — particularly in Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, and Tana River — depend on marine and coastal resources for their livelihoods, yet face persistent poverty, gender inequality, fragmented value chains, and escalating climate risks. Women and youth, who are central to coastal production systems, remain excluded from value chain governance, decision-making, and equitable economic returns. COSME Phase 1 established a strong foundation — building community trust, piloting women-led enterprise models and the Gender Justice Journey, initiating seaweed and mangrove-linked livelihoods, and generating evidence on what works. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical gaps: fragmented value chains, low production volumes, uneven institutional capacity, insufficient policy engagement, and gender participation that fell short of genuine transformation.

Goal and Objectives. COSME Phase 2 aims to improve the economic well-being, resilience, and agency of women and youth in coastal Kenya through inclusive, gender-transformative, and climate-resilient blue economy livelihoods, strengthened market systems, and accountable governance. The project pursues four interconnected objectives: (1) increasing climate-resilient coastal livelihood productivity and diversification; (2) strengthening women's economic empowerment, gender justice, and leadership; (3) facilitating inclusive market systems and value chain integration; and (4) building governance capacity, policy influence, and institutional sustainability.

Strategies and Pillars. The project employs a sequenced, layered implementation logic — capacity building → production → value addition → market integration → policy influence — delivered through a consortium of five partners (Plan International Kenya, MEDA, AECF, Natural State, CICaN). Plan International Kenya leads on gender-transformative programming, community institutional strengthening, safeguarding, evidence generation, and policy advocacy. MEDA and AECF lead on market systems and private sector partnerships. Natural State contributes conservation and production systems. CICaN supports institutional coordination. Shared strategic functions ensure coherence and amplify impact.

Target Groups and Geography. Primary beneficiaries are women and youth in coastal communities — members of women-led producer groups, cooperatives, VSLAs, WROs, YLOs, and BMUs. The project deepens engagement in Phase 1 geographies (Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, Tana River) with selective expansion.

Expected Outcomes. Increased and more resilient incomes; improved agency, leadership, and decision-making power of women; sustainable coastal ecosystem management; inclusive market integration; stronger community institutions; and evidence-informed, gender-responsive policy engagement.

Why Phase 2, Why Now. Kenya's devolution framework, growing blue economy discourse, and increased climate finance interest create a strategic window. COSME Phase 1's evidence, community trust, and established groups provide a launchpad for deeper, more systemic impact — bridging community-level innovation with system-level change for coastal women and youth.

5.1c Enumerator Conduct Requirements

  1. Safety First: Do not endanger yourself or respondents. If any situation feels unsafe, end the interview.
  2. Informed Consent: Always read the consent script completely. Never pressure anyone to participate.
  3. Confidentiality: Keep all data secure. Do not share individual responses. Use participant IDs, not names, in data systems.
  4. Sensitivity: Topics about gender, income, and safety are sensitive. Use empathetic tone. Watch for signs of distress.
  5. Privacy: Conduct interviews in private. Do not ask sensitive questions when others can hear.
  6. Vulnerable Populations: Extra care with minors (15-17), persons with disability, elderly, and GBV survivors. Follow safeguarding protocols.
  7. Referrals: If a respondent discloses abuse, exploitation, or immediate danger, follow the safeguarding referral pathway. Do NOT investigate yourself.
  8. Professional Conduct: Dress appropriately. Be respectful. Do not accept gifts or favors. Do not make promises about project benefits.
  9. Accuracy: Record responses exactly as given. Do not interpret or modify answers. Note any problems or uncertainties.
  10. Debriefing: Participate in daily debriefings with the field supervisor. Report any incidents, challenges, or safeguarding concerns immediately.

5.1d Gender-Sensitive Questioning Guidance

  1. Separate spaces: Interview women separately from men where possible, especially for sections on decision-making, agency, GBV, and care work.
  2. Same-sex enumerators: Where culturally appropriate, use female enumerators for women respondents on sensitive topics.
  3. Non-judgmental language: Avoid leading or judgmental phrasing. Use neutral, open-ended prompts.
  4. Power dynamics: Be aware of power dynamics between enumerator and respondent. Create a safe, equal-feeling space.
  5. Time sensitivity: Be mindful of women's time constraints (care work, enterprise). Schedule interviews at convenient times.
  6. Literacy levels: Adapt methods for respondents with limited literacy. Use visual aids where helpful.
  7. Cultural sensitivity: Respect local customs while maintaining ethical standards. Consult with community leaders on appropriate protocols.

5.1e Do-No-Harm & Trauma-Informed Data Collection

  1. Risk assessment: Before field deployment, assess potential risks to respondents and enumerators. Mitigate identified risks.
  2. Trigger awareness: Questions about income loss, climate shocks, gender violence, and exclusion may trigger emotional responses. Be prepared.
  3. Pause and support: If a respondent becomes distressed, pause the interview. Offer support. Allow them to decide whether to continue.
  4. No mandatory disclosure: Never require a respondent to disclose personal experiences of violence, abuse, or trauma.
  5. Referral pathway: Have a written referral pathway ready before fieldwork begins — including local health services, counseling, GBV response, and child protection contacts.
  6. Data security: Encrypt all digital data. Secure physical forms in locked storage. Limit access to named team members only.
  7. Anonymization: Remove identifying information before analysis and reporting. Use aggregate data in all external documents.
  8. Follow-up: For respondents who disclose safeguarding concerns, ensure follow-up through the safeguarding focal point within 24 hours.

5.1f Data Protection & Storage

  1. All digital data collected using KoboToolbox/ODK with encrypted transmission.
  2. Data stored on Plan International's secure servers with role-based access.
  3. Personal identifiers separated from survey responses and stored in a secure, password-protected file.
  4. Consent forms stored separately from data in locked physical storage.
  5. Data retention: Active project period + 5 years, then securely destroyed.
  6. Compliance with Kenya Data Protection Act 2019 and Plan International Data Protection Policy.
