Quick Synthesis of the Design

▼

1.1 Overall Vision

Plan International Canada, in partnership with Plan International Kenya, Cascadia Seaweed Corporation, CarbonAi, Verst Carbon, and MEDA, envisions this investment as a practical and commercially grounded solution that builds on GAC's prior investments (especially COSME and MEDA's LEGEND projects), turning coastal restoration and nature-based livelihoods into climate-resilient, inclusive, and durable economic growth. The project connects nature-based solutions (NbS), MSME acceleration, and policy operationalization, mobilizing rights-holders, county and national government, and the private sector. The theory of change is built on a mutually reinforcing coastal system strengthening approach where climate-smart seaweed enterprises provide fast-cycle income (especially for women and youth), restored mangroves protect farms, fisheries, and settlements while generating blue-carbon value, MSMEs aggregate, process, and market outputs from both systems, and carbon finance provides long-term incentives for conservation and co-management.

1.2 Key Development Challenges

  • Climate and ecological risk vs. blue economy potential — Climate impacts could reduce Kenya's GDP by 3.6—7.3% by 2050; coastal natural assets remain under-realized.
  • Mangrove communities lack viable pathways to restore, protect, and benefit from ecosystems — restoration cannot be sustained without alternative economic support, co-management, and credible pathways to ecosystem-service revenues.
  • Seaweed sector concentrated in low-value production — constrained by weak seed systems, limited processing capacity, fragmented regulatory environments, and insufficient market linkages.
  • Women and youth disproportionately excluded — insecure land tenure, limited access to finance and markets, unpaid care burdens, and elevated GBV risks constrain involvement.
  • Policy and institutional gaps — interagency coordination gaps, weak enforcement and compliance systems, and limited financial investment slow delivery and reduce predictability for MSMEs.
  • Carbon market readiness gaps — community awareness, FPIC, tenure/carbon-rights clarity, MRV credibility, benefit-sharing, and buyer confidence all require strengthening under Kenya's Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations 2024.
  • Fragmented value chains from Phase 1 — weak aggregation, quality standards, and market linkages; insufficient integration across production, value addition, and markets.
  • Gender participation without transformation — women participated but agency, leadership, and norm change were not systematically embedded at enterprise and governance levels.

1.2b Key Opportunities

  • Kenya's National Blue Economy Strategy 2025—2030 provides an integrated roadmap; National Landscape and Ecosystem Restoration Strategy targets 10.6M hectares by 2032.
  • Kenya's Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations 2024 create stronger enabling environment for blue-carbon participation.
  • COSME Phase 1 evidence base — rights-holders and local stakeholders are committed to nature-based enterprise growth; COSME reached >45,000 people, increased forest conservation group functionality by 32%, restored 63 hectares.
  • Seaweed biostimulants market projected to reach USD $4.4B by 2030 — Canadian-linked commercialization pathway through Cascadia.
  • Canadian expertise in dMRV and carbon markets through CarbonAi and Verst Carbon enables high-integrity voluntary carbon market access.
  • Gender Justice Journey model can be upgraded from household level to group and enterprise level; women comprise 90% of Kenya's seaweed workforce.
  • Reciprocal Canada-Kenya benefits — Canadian climate-service exports, regenerative agriculture innovation, and Ocean Supercluster alignment.
  • Partner complementarity — clear, non-duplicative roles across the six consortium members combining NbS, gender-transformation, enterprise development, carbon readiness, and seaweed commercialization.

Approved Title
Catalyzing Inclusive, Climate-Resilient and Sustainable Blue Economies for Kenya's Coastal Communities

2.2 Background and Rationale

Development Challenge

Kenya's coast faces a dual reality of rising climate and ecological risk alongside significant under-realized blue economy potential. The World Bank estimates that climate impacts could reduce Kenya's GDP by 3.6—7.3% by 2050. Fisheries and aquaculture contribute <1% of GDP while supporting >4 million Kenyans, and underexplored areas such as seaweed farming present pathways to foster economic contribution. Canada has been a core partner to Kenya in strengthening an inclusive blue economy, and this is a critical moment to consolidate GAC's investments and advance Canada—Africa Strategy development-trade objectives.

Three Linked Development Challenges

First, mangrove-dependent coastal communities still lack viable, inclusive pathways to restore, protect, and benefit from ecosystems. Kenya's mangrove landscapes cover over 61,000 hectares, yet restoration will not be sustained without viable alternative economic support, strengthened co-management, and credible pathways to ecosystem-service revenues. Kenya's Climate Change (Carbon Markets) Regulations 2024, together with the operationalization of Kenya's carbon-market architecture, create a stronger enabling environment for blue-carbon participation — but effective participation still depends on community awareness, FPIC, tenure/carbon-rights clarity, credible MRV, benefit-sharing arrangements, and buyer confidence.

Second, seaweed remains one of the clearest inclusive blue economy opportunities, but the sector is concentrated in low-value production. UNCTAD notes value capture is commonly constrained by high compliance costs, limited certification, weak processing capacity, and fragmented regulatory environments. COSME's 2025 midline assessment points to next-level bottlenecks across seed systems, post-harvest quality control, aggregation, value addition, market linkages, and access to fit-for-purpose finance.

Third, Kenya's policy and institutional architecture is advancing in real time. The recently launched National Blue Economy Strategy 2025—2030 provides an integrated roadmap, while the National Landscape and Ecosystem Restoration Strategy targets 10.6M hectares by 2032. However, interagency coordination gaps, weak enforcement and compliance systems, and limited financial investment slow delivery and constrain participation of women and youth.

Phase 1 Achievements and Key Learnings

COSME Phase 1 (2023—2027) established a solid implementation foundation, reaching >45,000 people through NbS and climate action, increasing forest conservation group functionality by 32%, restoring 63 hectares of forest, and enhancing women's household, group and enterprise decision-making by 38%. Phase 1 demonstrated that women-led enterprises thrive when supported holistically, built community trust, piloted the Gender Justice Journey at household level, and generated an evidence base on coastal livelihoods and gender dynamics. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical limitations requiring the next phase of investment.

Solution Approach

This investment presents a practical and commercially grounded solution that connects nature-based solutions (NbS), MSME acceleration, and policy operationalization, mobilizing rights-holders, county and national government, and the private sector. The consortium combines community engagement in NbS, gender-transformative programming, and policy operationalization (Plan International), technical support for enterprise and market development (MEDA), and specialized Canadian and Kenyan private sector expertise in seaweed commercialization (Cascadia) and carbon-market readiness (CarbonAi and Verst Carbon).

2.4 Theory of Change

a) Narrative Theory of Change

The project's theory of change is built on a mutually reinforcing coastal system strengthening approach: Kenya's coastal communities can achieve resilient and higher-value livelihoods if three conditions are pursued together: (i) natural assets that underpin coastal economies must be restored and governed in ways that make conservation economically rational; (ii) livelihood sectors with strong potential, especially seaweed, must be upgraded from low-value, vulnerable production to climate-resilient and commercially viable value chains; (iii) policy, regulatory, and coordination bottlenecks that suppress carbon-linked PES and MSME growth must be reduced for successful local models to scale.

If community-based ecological mangrove restoration (CBEMR) is implemented across 1,300 hectares aligned with County restoration plans, and if inclusive, gender-responsive co-management governance structures are strengthened, then targeted ecosystems will be ecologically restored and effectively managed. If blue-carbon readiness systems, MRV infrastructure, and financing arrangements are established in compliance with Kenya's Climate Change Act and Carbon Market Regulations, then the pathway to high-integrity voluntary carbon markets with transparent benefit-sharing becomes viable. Simultaneously, if reliable seed systems, climate-resilient production, and post-harvest quality are established, and if enterprises are formalized, upgraded, and connected to gender-responsive markets, finance, and BDS, then a climate-resilient, market-driven seaweed industry will emerge. And if multi-stakeholder coordination mechanisms address regulatory bottlenecks and evidence-based advocacy strengthens policy processes, then the enabling environment will support scaling of both restoration and enterprise models.

2.5 Target Groups and Coverage

Primary Target Groups

  • Women in coastal communities: Members of women-led producer groups, cooperatives, VSLAs, WROs, and BMUs engaged in seaweed, mangrove-linked, and conservation enterprises.
  • Youth (focus on young women and adolescent girls): Members of YLOs, youth enterprise groups, and blue economy pathway participants.
  • Women-led and youth-led enterprises: Groups and cooperatives producing, processing, and trading coastal products.

Secondary / Indirect Target Groups

  • Households of primary participants.
  • Community leaders, men, and boys engaged through GJJ.
  • Private sector actors in value addition and market access.
  • County and national government institutions.
  • Community-based conservation groups and BMUs.

Geographic Focus

  • Core counties: Kilifi and Kwale (approximately 1,300 hectares of mangrove ecosystems).
  • Strategy: Deepen in Phase 1 geographies; scaling across Kwale, Kilifi, and neighbouring counties where models are proven.
  • Clustering: Groups clustered for peer learning, aggregation, and market viability.

Collaboration & Systems-Strengthening Architecture

5.1 Consortium Collaboration Model

COSME 2.0 is implemented by a six-partner consortium combining Canadian technical innovation with Kenyan community-rooted delivery. The model integrates geographic coordination with technical specialization across three interdependent components. Plan International Canada provides overall project leadership, fiduciary management, and GAC relationship management. Plan International Kenya serves as the in-country lead, coordinating field implementation and government engagement. Cascadia Seaweed brings Canadian seaweed research, seed science, and biostimulant innovation. CarbonAi delivers digital MRV (dMRV) technology and Canadian carbon market access. Verst Carbon Corporation provides blue-carbon project development, validation/registration, and carbon credit commercialization expertise. MEDA drives market systems development, inclusive finance, and private-sector engagement for the seaweed value chain.

The consortium operates through a layered governance structure: a Steering Committee provides strategic oversight; a Technical Advisory Group guides cross-cutting research, carbon methodology, and policy questions; and Component Working Groups coordinate implementation. Plan International Kenya plays a convening role, promoting alignment, trust-building, and connecting technical partners with community actors and government institutions across Kilifi and Kwale counties.

Boundaries, Limitations & Assumptions

▼

6.1 Scope Boundaries

  • Thematic focus: Inclusive blue economies — mangrove NbS and carbon finance, seaweed value chain development, and enabling policy/coordination. Not broad livelihood diversification.
  • Target group: Women and youth in coastal communities — members of producer groups, cooperatives, BMUs/CFAs, WROs, YLOs. Not general population.
  • Market engagement: Facilitation of market access through MEDA, Cascadia, CarbonAi, Verst Carbon and private sector — Plan does not operate as a commercial entity.
  • Geographic scope: Kilifi and Kwale counties (1,300 ha mangrove restoration; seaweed production sites along the coast).
  • Carbon pathway: Phased readiness approach — feasibility and dMRV in Years 1—2, documentation/validation in Years 3—4, market access and initial credit issuance in Years 5—6.

6.2 Realistic Change Ambition

✅ What the project WILL achieve

  • Inclusive market participation for women- and youth-led enterprises.
  • Measurable improvements in women's agency, leadership, and decision-making.
  • Stronger, accountable community institutions linked to county structures.
  • Evidence-based policy influence at county level, with national entry points.
  • Operational safeguarding, GESI, and learning systems across the consortium.

What remains BEYOND scope

  • Full transformation of national export markets.
  • Systemic resolution of all climate risks.
  • Complete national-level policy reform on blue economy and gender.
  • Reaching all coastal communities across Kenya's entire coastline.
  • Replacing government or private sector roles.

6.3 Key Assumptions

  • Government support: Continued political will in blue economy and devolution processes.
  • Climate manageability: Climate shocks remain within adaptable levels.
  • Political stability: Minimum stability enables program operations and policy dialogue.
  • Partner continuity: All six consortium partners maintain capacity and commitment.
  • Community willingness: Target communities remain engaged in gender-transformative programming.
  • Market viability: Sufficient demand for coastal products.
  • Civic space: CSOs, WROs, YLOs can continue to organize and advocate.
  • Donor alignment: Donor expectations align with systems-strengthening and adaptive management approach.

Executive Summary

▼

Catalyzing Inclusive, Sustainable Blue Economies for Kenya's Coastal Communities (COSME 2.0)

Background and Problem. Kenya's coastal communities — particularly in Kilifi and Kwale counties — depend on marine and coastal resources for their livelihoods, yet face persistent poverty, gender inequality, fragmented value chains, degraded ecosystems, and escalating climate risks. Women and youth, who are central to coastal production systems, remain excluded from value chain governance, decision-making, and equitable economic returns. Mangrove ecosystems — critical for carbon sequestration, biodiversity, and coastal resilience — continue to degrade under pressure from overexploitation, pollution, and climate change. COSME Phase 1 established a strong foundation — building community trust, piloting women-led enterprise models and the Gender Justice Journey, initiating seaweed and mangrove-linked livelihoods, and generating evidence on what works. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical gaps: fragmented value chains, low production volumes, insufficient carbon finance readiness, uneven institutional capacity, and gender participation that fell short of genuine transformation.

Goal and Objectives. COSME 2.0 aims to achieve strengthened inclusive, climate-resilient and sustainable blue economies for Kenya's coastal communities through three interconnected components (GAC RBM style): Component 1 (IO 1100) — Inclusive Nature-based Market Opportunities, covering mangrove restoration, inclusive co-management governance, and blue-carbon readiness/market engagement via dMRV; Component 2 (IO 1200) — Climate-Resilient Seaweed Industry, covering production/seed systems, enterprise strengthening/sea-to-field value chains, and market access/inclusive finance; and Component 3 (IO 1300) — Enabling Policy, Regulatory & Coordination Environment, covering policy dialogue, advocacy, and stakeholder capacity.

Strategy and Consortium. The project employs a sequenced, layered implementation logic — restoration → co-management → carbon market readiness (Component 1); seed supply → production → value addition → market integration (Component 2); and evidence → dialogue → policy influence (Component 3). A six-partner consortium delivers the program: Plan International Canada (project leadership, fiduciary management, GAC relations), Plan International Kenya (in-country lead, gender transformation, community governance, safeguarding, MEL, policy advocacy), Cascadia Seaweed (Canadian seaweed R&D, seed science, biostimulant innovation), CarbonAi (digital MRV, Canadian carbon market access), Verst Carbon Corporation (blue-carbon project development, validation/registration, commercialization), and MEDA (market systems, inclusive finance, private-sector engagement).

Target Groups and Geography. Primary beneficiaries are women and youth in coastal communities of Kilifi and Kwale counties — members of producer groups, cooperatives, BMUs/CFAs, WROs, YLOs, and VSLAs. The project targets approximately 1,300 hectares of mangrove restoration and conservation, and expands climate-resilient seaweed production across priority coastal sites.

Expected Outcomes. Increased and more resilient incomes; improved agency, leadership, and decision-making power of women; sustainable coastal ecosystem management with carbon finance readiness; inclusive market integration for seaweed and nature-based enterprises; stronger community co-management institutions; and an enabling policy environment shaped by evidence and community voice.

Project Parameters. Duration: 6 years. Total budget: CAD $40 million. Geographic focus: Kilifi and Kwale counties. Blue-carbon phasing: readiness (Years 1—2), documentation/validation (Years 3—4), market access and initial credit issuance (Years 5—6). Canadian reciprocal benefits include CarbonAi dMRV platform advancement, Cascadia seaweed product innovation, and Canada's Ocean Supercluster contributions.

5.1c Enumerator Conduct Requirements

  1. Safety First: Do not endanger yourself or respondents. If any situation feels unsafe, end the interview.
  2. Informed Consent: Always read the consent script completely. Never pressure anyone to participate.
  3. Confidentiality: Keep all data secure. Do not share individual responses. Use participant IDs, not names, in data systems.
  4. Sensitivity: Topics about gender, income, and safety are sensitive. Use empathetic tone. Watch for signs of distress.
  5. Privacy: Conduct interviews in private. Do not ask sensitive questions when others can hear.
  6. Vulnerable Populations: Extra care with minors (15-17), persons with disability, elderly, and GBV survivors. Follow safeguarding protocols.
  7. Referrals: If a respondent discloses abuse, exploitation, or immediate danger, follow the safeguarding referral pathway. Do NOT investigate yourself.
  8. Professional Conduct: Dress appropriately. Be respectful. Do not accept gifts or favors. Do not make promises about project benefits.
  9. Accuracy: Record responses exactly as given. Do not interpret or modify answers. Note any problems or uncertainties.
  10. Debriefing: Participate in daily debriefings with the field supervisor. Report any incidents, challenges, or safeguarding concerns immediately.

5.1d Gender-Sensitive Questioning Guidance

  1. Separate spaces: Interview women separately from men where possible, especially for sections on decision-making, agency, GBV, and care work.
  2. Same-sex enumerators: Where culturally appropriate, use female enumerators for women respondents on sensitive topics.
  3. Non-judgmental language: Avoid leading or judgmental phrasing. Use neutral, open-ended prompts.
  4. Power dynamics: Be aware of power dynamics between enumerator and respondent. Create a safe, equal-feeling space.
  5. Time sensitivity: Be mindful of women's time constraints (care work, enterprise). Schedule interviews at convenient times.
  6. Literacy levels: Adapt methods for respondents with limited literacy. Use visual aids where helpful.
  7. Cultural sensitivity: Respect local customs while maintaining ethical standards. Consult with community leaders on appropriate protocols.

5.1e Do-No-Harm & Trauma-Informed Data Collection

  1. Risk assessment: Before field deployment, assess potential risks to respondents and enumerators. Mitigate identified risks.
  2. Trigger awareness: Questions about income loss, climate shocks, gender violence, and exclusion may trigger emotional responses. Be prepared.
  3. Pause and support: If a respondent becomes distressed, pause the interview. Offer support. Allow them to decide whether to continue.
  4. No mandatory disclosure: Never require a respondent to disclose personal experiences of violence, abuse, or trauma.
  5. Referral pathway: Have a written referral pathway ready before fieldwork begins — including local health services, counseling, GBV response, and child protection contacts.
  6. Data security: Encrypt all digital data. Secure physical forms in locked storage. Limit access to named team members only.
  7. Anonymization: Remove identifying information before analysis and reporting. Use aggregate data in all external documents.
  8. Follow-up: For respondents who disclose safeguarding concerns, ensure follow-up through the safeguarding focal point within 24 hours.

5.1f Data Protection & Storage

  1. All digital data collected using KoboToolbox/ODK with encrypted transmission.
  2. Data stored on Plan International's secure servers with role-based access.
  3. Personal identifiers separated from survey responses and stored in a secure, password-protected file.
  4. Consent forms stored separately from data in locked physical storage.
  5. Data retention: Active project period + 5 years, then securely destroyed.
  6. Compliance with Kenya Data Protection Act 2019 and Plan International Data Protection Policy.
