# 1. QUICK SYNTHESIS OF SOURCE TEXT

## 1.1 Overall Vision (Summary Paragraph)

Plan International Kenya envisions COSME Phase 2 as a transformative evolution from a strong implementation consortium into a **cohesive, learning-oriented, and influence-driven partnership** that delivers measurable improvements in women's and youth's lives, coastal ecosystem sustainability, and governance systems. The collaboration logic centres on a **hybrid model** combining geographic coordination with technical specialization: Plan International Kenya leads in established geographies with strengths in gender justice, regenerative agriculture, enterprise development, and conservation governance, while MEDA, AECF, REAsee, and CICaN contribute complementary technical expertise in processing, value addition, market access, and institutional coordination. All five partners share strategic functions — research, MEL, policy advocacy, safeguarding, and institutional strengthening — to ensure coherence, reduce duplication, and amplify collective impact through sequenced and layered interventions across the value chain.

## 1.2 Key Problems / Gaps from Phase 1

- **Fragmented value chains and low production volumes** — interventions were insufficiently integrated across production, value addition, and markets.
- **Uneven institutional capacity** among women- and youth-led groups, limiting sustainability and scale readiness.
- **Gaps between community-level practice and policy frameworks** — limited translation of community innovation into governance and budget influence.
- **Livelihood gains alone proved insufficient** without parallel governance and institutional change.
- **Gender participation without transformation** — women participated but agency, leadership, and norm change were not systematically embedded.
- **Disconnected interventions** — activities lacked the sequenced, layered logic (capacity → production → value addition → market → policy) needed for durable impact.
- **Institutional fragmentation** and limited local capacity among coordination platforms and government forums.
- **Safeguarding and GESI mainstreaming** not yet systematically embedded across all partner activities.

## 1.2b Key Opportunities for Phase 2

- **Strong potential for women-led enterprises** when supported holistically (demonstrated in Phase 1).
- **Devolution** creates county-level entry points for policy engagement, budgeting, and blue economy governance.
- **Growing blue economy discourse** nationally and regionally increases political will and donor interest.
- **Increased climate finance interest** offers new resource mobilization and co-financing opportunities.
- **Established trust and community systems** from Phase 1 provide a foundation for deepening rather than starting from scratch.
- **COSME Phase 1 evidence base** can be leveraged for advocacy, adaptive programming, and influence.
- **Partner complementarity** — clear, non-duplicative roles across the five consortium members enable layered, systems-level programming.
- **Gender Justice Journey model** can be upgraded from household level to group and enterprise level.

## 2.2 Background and Rationale

### Context of Coastal Communities and Blue Economy Opportunities
Kenya's coastal counties — including Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, and Tana River — are home to communities whose livelihoods depend on marine and coastal resources, including seaweed farming, mangrove-linked enterprises, and small-scale fisheries. Despite the significant economic potential of the blue economy, coastal communities face persistent poverty, gender inequality, limited market access, and escalating climate risks. Women and youth, who form the backbone of coastal production systems, remain disproportionately excluded from decision-making, value chain governance, and equitable economic returns. Kenya's devolution framework and the growing national discourse on the blue economy present an unprecedented opportunity to address these structural barriers at county and national levels.

### Phase 1 Achievements and Key Learnings
COSME Phase 1 established a solid implementation foundation, demonstrating that women-led enterprises thrive when supported holistically with production inputs, savings (VSLAs), skills development, and mentorship. Phase 1 built community trust, established women- and youth-led groups, piloted the Gender Justice Journey at household level, initiated seaweed and mangrove-linked livelihoods, and generated an evidence base on coastal livelihoods and gender dynamics. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical limitations: fragmented value chains, low production volumes, uneven institutional capacity among community groups, and insufficient linkage between community innovation and policy influence.

### Gaps and Challenges Justifying Phase 2
Despite Phase 1 progress, fundamental gaps persist. Value chains remain fragmented, with weak aggregation, quality standards, and market linkages. Community institutions — WROs, YLOs, cooperatives — have variable governance, financial management, and sustainability capacity. Gender participation has not yet translated into systematic norm change, agency, or leadership at enterprise and governance levels. Policy engagement remains ad hoc, with insufficient use of evidence to influence county and national budgets and plans. Safeguarding systems require strengthening across the consortium. These gaps underscore the need for a dedicated second phase that integrates livelihoods, gender transformation, market systems, and governance into a coherent, layered, and systems-oriented program.

### Why a Gender-Transformative, Systems-Strengthening, and Market Systems Approach Is Needed
Addressing poverty and inequality in coastal Kenya requires more than livelihood support; it demands transformation of the systems — social, economic, institutional, and political — that perpetuate exclusion. A **gender-transformative approach** moves beyond women's access to resources to tackle harmful norms, power relations, unpaid care work, and decision-making barriers at household, group, enterprise, and governance levels. A **systems-strengthening approach** builds the capacity, accountability, and sustainability of community institutions, government forums, and coordination platforms to sustain gains beyond the project cycle. A **market systems approach** facilitates — rather than replaces — private sector linkages, enabling women and youth to participate in markets on equitable terms. Together, these approaches ensure that COSME Phase 2 delivers not only economic gains but also durable, inclusive, and systemic change.

## 2.3 Overall Goal and Specific Objectives

### Overall Goal (Impact Level)
**To improve the economic well-being, resilience, and agency of women and youth in coastal Kenya through inclusive, gender-transformative, and climate-resilient blue economy livelihoods, strengthened market systems, and accountable governance.**

### Specific Objectives

**Specific Objective 1: Climate-Resilient Coastal Livelihoods**
To increase the productivity, diversification, and climate resilience of coastal livelihood systems — particularly seaweed, mangrove-linked, and conservation-based enterprises — for women and youth in target communities.

**Specific Objective 2: Women's Economic Empowerment and Gender Justice**
To strengthen the economic agency, leadership, decision-making power, and collective action of women and girls in coastal communities through gender-transformative programming, including VSLAs, enterprise governance, and the upgraded Gender Justice Journey.

**Specific Objective 3: Market Systems and Value Chain Strengthening**
To facilitate improved value addition, quality standards, aggregation, and inclusive market linkages for coastal products, enabling women- and youth-led enterprises to participate equitably in local, regional, and national markets.

**Specific Objective 4: Governance, Policy Influence, and Institutional Capacity**
To strengthen community institutions (WROs, YLOs, cooperatives, BMUs), coordination platforms, and evidence-based policy engagement on blue economy, climate adaptation, and gender-responsive governance at county and national levels.

## 2.4 Theory of Change (ToC)

### a) Narrative Theory of Change

COSME Phase 2 is premised on the understanding that sustainable, inclusive economic empowerment for coastal women and youth requires simultaneous and sequenced investment across four interconnected domains: climate-resilient production, gender-transformative empowerment, inclusive market systems, and accountable governance. **If** women and youth receive sustained support in climate-smart production, business skills, and financial services (VSLAs), **and if** gender-transformative programming (the upgraded Gender Justice Journey) addresses norms, power relations, and unpaid care at group and enterprise levels, **then** women and youth will increase their productivity, diversify income, and assume leadership in economic and governance spaces. **If** value chain interventions strengthen aggregation, quality standards, and market access through partnerships with MEDA, AECF, and CICaN, **then** women- and youth-led enterprises will capture greater value and become more resilient. **If** community institutions are strengthened in governance, financial management, and accountability, **and if** evidence is generated and used for coordinated advocacy at county and national levels, **then** policy frameworks and public investments will become more inclusive and responsive. Together, these pathways contribute to the long-term impact of improved well-being, resilience, and gender justice for coastal communities, with gains sustained through strengthened systems and institutions.

Gender justice, safeguarding, and systems-strengthening are not add-ons but are embedded throughout the causal chain: safeguarding standards protect participants at every stage; GESI markers and indicators are applied across all consortium activities; and learning loops ensure that evidence continuously informs adaptive programming and advocacy.

- Community institutions strengthened in governance, transparency, and sustainability.
- Research conducted and learning products disseminated; safeguarding systems operational.

**WHICH LEADS TO (Intermediate Outcomes):**
- Increased and more resilient incomes for women and youth from coastal enterprises.
- Improved agency, leadership, and decision-making power of women in economic and governance spaces.

- Evidence-informed policy engagement and responsive governance frameworks.

**CONTRIBUTING TO (Long-Term Impact):**
- Improved economic well-being, resilience, and gender justice for coastal women, youth, and their communities.
- Inclusive, sustainable blue economy governance and policy at county and national levels.
- Systems-level change sustained beyond the project through strengthened institutions.

## 2.5 Target Groups and Coverage

### Primary Target Groups
- **Women in coastal communities**: Members of women-led producer groups, cooperatives, VSLAs, WROs, and Beach Management Units (BMUs) engaged in seaweed, mangrove-linked, and conservation enterprises.
- **Youth (with focus on young women and adolescent girls)**: Members of youth-led organizations (YLOs), youth enterprise groups, and young participants in blue economy pathways (skills development, mentoring, safe economic participation).
- **Women-led and youth-led enterprises**: Groups and cooperatives producing, processing, and trading coastal products.

### Secondary / Indirect Target Groups
- **Households** of primary participants (benefiting from increased income, improved gender dynamics, and reduced harmful norms).
- **Community leaders, men, and boys** engaged through Gender Justice Journey and social norms programming.
- **Private sector actors** partnering in value addition, market access, and enterprise development.
- **County and national government institutions** engaged in policy dialogue, planning, and blue economy governance.
- **Community-based conservation groups** and BMUs involved in ecosystem management.

### Proposed Geographic Focus
- **Core counties**: Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, Tana River (coastal Kenya).
- **Strategy**: Deepen engagement in existing Phase 1 geographies where trust and systems are established, with **selective expansion** into new areas based on ecological suitability, governance readiness, and partner presence.
- **Clustering**: Groups will be clustered to enable peer learning, aggregation, and market viability.

### Deepening vs. Expansion
- **Primary emphasis**: Deepening engagement with existing women- and youth-led groups — strengthening resilience, leadership, production, income diversification, and decision-making power.
- **Secondary emphasis**: Selective geographic expansion where ecological and governance conditions support it, avoiding over-extension.

# 5. COLLABORATION & SYSTEMS-STRENGTHENING ARCHITECTURE

## 5.1 Consortium Collaboration Model

COSME Phase 2 adopts a **hybrid collaboration model** that combines **geographic coordination with technical specialization** and **shared strategic functions**. Each partner leads in specific geographies where they have established trust and operational systems, while contributing distinct technical strengths that are sequenced and layered across the value chain. This means that interventions are not delivered in parallel silos but are intentionally connected: communities move from capacity building → production → value addition → market integration → policy influence, with different partners contributing at different stages. The model avoids duplication by clearly delineating lead and support roles for each intervention area.

Critical cross-cutting components — including research, MEL, learning, policy advocacy, safeguarding, and institutional strengthening — are **jointly designed and delivered** by all five partners to ensure coherence and amplify collective impact. Joint planning and reflection spaces, harmonized tools and standards (GESI, safeguarding, MEL), and shared learning platforms ensure that the consortium operates as a **cohesive, learning-oriented, and influence-driven partnership** rather than a collection of independent implementers. Plan International Kenya plays a soft leadership role in consortium coordination, promoting alignment and trust-building, while acting as a connector between technical partners, community actors, and government institutions.

# 6. BOUNDARIES, LIMITATIONS & ASSUMPTIONS

## 6.1 Scope Boundaries

- **Thematic focus**: COSME Phase 2 concentrates on a defined set of **coastal and marine-based livelihood systems** (seaweed, mangrove-linked, conservation-based enterprises) rather than broad livelihood diversification or non-coastal sectors.
- **Target group focus**: The project works strategically with **existing women- and youth-led groups** (WROs, YLOs, VSLAs, cooperatives, BMUs) established or identified in Phase 1, **clustered** to enable peer learning, aggregation, and market viability. It does not target the general population or all coastal residents.
- **Market engagement limits**: Plan International Kenya and consortium partners **facilitate** access to markets through private sector partnerships (MEDA, AECF), social enterprises, and buyer linkages — but do not directly operate as a commercial entity or trader.
- **Geographic scope**: Deepening in existing Phase 1 geographies with selective expansion where ecological suitability, governance readiness, and partner presence support it. Not a blanket geographic expansion.

## 6.2 Realistic Change Ambition

**What the project will reasonably achieve within Phase 2 (4 years):**
- Inclusive market participation for women- and youth-led enterprises in defined coastal value chains.
- Measurable improvements in women's economic agency, leadership, and decision-making power at group, enterprise, and community governance levels.
- Stronger, more accountable community institutions with improved governance, financial management, and linkages to county structures.
- Evidence-based policy influence on blue economy, climate adaptation, and gender-responsive governance at county level, with entry points at national level.
- Operational safeguarding, GESI, and learning systems across the consortium.

**What remains beyond scope / beyond one phase:**
- Full transformation of national export markets or national blue economy value chains.
- Systemic resolution of all climate risks facing coastal communities.
- Complete national-level policy reform on blue economy and gender (though advocacy and evidence aim to catalyse this).
- Reaching all coastal communities across Kenya's entire coastline.
- Replacing government or private sector roles in market provisioning and governance.

## 6.3 Key Assumptions

- **Government support**: Continued political will and government engagement in blue economy and devolution processes (CIDPs, sector plans, budgets) at county and national levels.
- **Climate manageability**: Climate shocks remain within manageable levels that allow adaptation and do not destroy productive assets or displace communities entirely.
- **Political stability**: Minimum political stability in target counties enabling program operations, community engagement, and policy dialogue.
- **Partner continuity**: All five consortium partners maintain operational capacity, commitment, and funding throughout Phase 2.
- **Community willingness**: Target communities and groups remain willing to engage, adopt new practices, and participate in gender-transformative programming.
- **Market viability**: Sufficient and stable demand exists for coastal products (seaweed, mangrove-linked products) to reward enterprise investment.
- **Civic space**: Civil society organizations, WROs, and YLOs continue to have sufficient civic space to advocate, organize, and engage with government.
- **Donor alignment**: Donor expectations and reporting requirements are aligned with the systems-strengthening and gender-transformative approach, allowing adaptive management.

# 7. MEL, RESEARCH, LEARNING & ADAPTIVE MANAGEMENT

## 7.1 MEL Approach

COSME Phase 2 MEL will be **harmonized, participatory, and gender-sensitive**, ensuring that monitoring, evaluation, and learning serve both accountability and adaptive programming.

**Key features:**
- **Harmonized indicators and joint learning** across all five COSME partners, with a shared MEL framework, common data collection tools, and unified reporting to enable cross-consortium comparison and aggregation.
- **Outcome harvesting** to capture emergent, unintended, and systemic outcomes — particularly changes in gender norms, institutional capacity, and policy influence — that standard indicator tracking may miss.
- **Participatory monitoring** engaging women and youth groups in data collection, reflection, and feedback, ensuring that communities are not only subjects but active agents in monitoring and learning.
- **Feedback loops** from women and youth groups to program design and management, including structured community feedback and complaint mechanisms to inform course corrections.
- **GESI markers and indicators** applied across all consortium activities, tracking both economic dimensions (income, assets, market access) and gender-transformative dimensions (agency, decision-making, norms, leadership, safeguarding).
- **Safeguarding monitoring** integrated into the MEL system, aligned with Plan International's child and participant safeguarding commitments.
- **Data disaggregation** by sex, age, county, value chain, and group type as standard practice.
- **Quarterly and annual reflection** events across the consortium to review data, adapt programming, and document learning.

## 7.2 Research & Learning Agenda

**Research Priorities:**
- **Participatory action research** on gender, climate resilience, and coastal livelihoods — conducted with and for women- and youth-led groups, not extractive.
- **Evidence generation on what works** for women-led blue economy enterprises and youth-led groups — identifying success factors, barriers, and scalable models.
- **Social norms research** — understanding how norms, power relations, and unpaid care work interact with economic empowerment at group and enterprise levels.
- **Climate resilience research** — documenting adaptive strategies, risk reduction innovations, and ecosystem co-benefits of livelihoods interventions.

**Learning Products:**
- Case studies, stories of change, good practice briefs, and policy-relevant research reports.
- Community-generated evidence (photo-voice, community scorecards, most significant change stories).

**Learning Loops and Adaptation Mechanisms:**
- Findings from research and MEL are systematically fed back into programming through quarterly reflection meetings, annual learning reviews, and adaptive management protocols.
- A **learning agenda** is maintained and updated annually, with priorities emerging from both planned research and outcome harvesting.
- Community and partner capacity is strengthened to use data for decision-making, not just reporting.

## 7.3 Evidence-Based Advocacy Strategy

**Priority Advocacy Themes:**
- Blue economy governance, policy, and financing (county and national).
- Climate adaptation and resilience in coastal livelihoods.
- Gender-responsive policies, budgets, and institutional frameworks.
- Inclusive financing models for women- and youth-led enterprises.

**County-Level Entry Points:**
- County Integrated Development Plans (CIDPs) and sector plans.
- County budget processes (advocacy for blue economy and gender-responsive allocations).
- County blue economy and environment committees and forums.

**National-Level Entry Points:**
- National Blue Economy Strategy and related policy processes.
- National climate change adaptation and gender policy frameworks.
- Parliamentary committees and national-level sector working groups.

**Coalitions and Platforms:**
- Leverage and strengthen existing government-led coordination platforms (not creating parallel ones).
- Support structured engagement between communities, county government, and national institutions.
- Build coalitions with other CSOs, research institutions, and private sector actors.

**Role of WROs, YLOs, and Community Voices:**
- Women and youth are not only beneficiaries but are active advocates — their voices, evidence, and experiences are amplified in policy spaces.
- WROs and YLOs are supported to engage directly in county and national policy dialogue.
- Community-level evidence is translated into policy-relevant messaging.
- Partners coordinate advocacy to avoid fragmented or competing approaches.

# 9. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## COSME Phase 2: Advancing Climate-Resilient Blue Economy Livelihoods, Gender Justice, and Systems Change for Coastal Women and Youth in Kenya

**Background and Problem.** Coastal communities in Kenya — particularly in Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, and Tana River — depend on marine and coastal resources for their livelihoods, yet face persistent poverty, gender inequality, fragmented value chains, and escalating climate risks. Women and youth, who are central to coastal production systems, remain excluded from value chain governance, decision-making, and equitable economic returns. COSME Phase 1 established a strong foundation — building community trust, piloting women-led enterprise models and the Gender Justice Journey, initiating seaweed and mangrove-linked livelihoods, and generating evidence on what works. However, Phase 1 also revealed critical gaps: fragmented value chains, low production volumes, uneven institutional capacity, insufficient policy engagement, and gender participation that fell short of genuine transformation.

**Goal and Objectives.** COSME Phase 2 aims to **improve the economic well-being, resilience, and agency of women and youth in coastal Kenya** through inclusive, gender-transformative, and climate-resilient blue economy livelihoods, strengthened market systems, and accountable governance. The project pursues four interconnected objectives: (1) increasing climate-resilient coastal livelihood productivity and diversification; (2) strengthening women's economic empowerment, gender justice, and leadership; (3) facilitating inclusive market systems and value chain integration; and (4) building governance capacity, policy influence, and institutional sustainability.

**Strategies and Pillars.** The project employs a **sequenced, layered implementation logic** — capacity building → production → value addition → market integration → policy influence — delivered through a consortium of five partners (Plan International Kenya, MEDA, AECF, REAsee, CICaN). Plan International Kenya leads on gender-transformative programming (upgrading the Gender Justice Journey to group and enterprise level), women's and youth empowerment, community institutional strengthening, safeguarding, evidence generation, and policy advocacy. MEDA and AECF lead on market systems, value addition, and private sector partnerships. REAsee contributes production systems and technical training. CICaN supports institutional coordination and governance platforms. Shared strategic functions — research, MEL, learning, advocacy, and safeguarding — are co-designed and co-delivered to ensure coherence and amplify impact.

**Target Groups and Geography.** The primary beneficiaries are **women and youth in coastal communities** — members of women-led producer groups, cooperatives, VSLAs, WROs, YLOs, and BMUs engaged in seaweed, mangrove-linked, and conservation enterprises. The project deepens engagement in existing Phase 1 geographies (Kilifi, Kwale, Lamu, Tana River) with selective expansion based on ecological suitability and governance readiness.

**Expected Outcomes.** By the end of Phase 2, the project expects: increased and more resilient incomes for women and youth; improved agency, leadership, and decision-making power of women; more sustainable management of coastal ecosystems; women- and youth-led enterprises integrated into inclusive markets; stronger, more accountable community institutions linked to county structures; and evidence-informed, gender-responsive policy engagement at county and national levels.

**Collaboration and Systems Change.** The consortium's hybrid model ensures complementarity, not duplication — combining geographic coordination, technical specialization, and shared strategic functions. The project invests in systems strengthening: building institutions, platforms, and policy pathways that will sustain gains beyond the project. Learning, safeguarding, and gender justice are not add-ons but are embedded across all activities and partnerships.

**Why Phase 2, Why Now.** Kenya's devolution framework, growing blue economy discourse, and increased climate finance interest create a strategic window. COSME Phase 1's evidence base, community trust, and established groups provide a launchpad for deeper, more systemic impact. Phase 2 is an opportunity to bridge community-level innovation with system-level change — delivering not just more, but better, more inclusive, and more durable results for coastal women and youth.
